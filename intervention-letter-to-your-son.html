How to Write an Intervention Letter to Your Son (With a Real Example)

TheFirstWord

It's late, the house is quiet, and you're staring at a blank page. You know what you want him to understand. You have no idea how to say it without him crumpling the letter — or the relationship — in the first paragraph. Here's how a letter like this actually gets written, with an example you can learn from.

The short version: a letter works where talking fails because it can't be interrupted. It's everything you'd say if he ever let you finish a sentence — which is exactly why the first line matters more than all the others.

Why a letter reaches him when talking doesn't

Every conversation about his use so far has probably followed the same script: you start, he gets defensive, voices rise, someone leaves the room. A letter breaks that script. He can't talk over a page. He can read it at 2 a.m. when the defensiveness is down. And he can read it twice — which people do, almost always, with letters like this. You're not choosing a letter because it's easier for you. You're choosing it because it's harder for him to escape.

Open with a memory, not a charge sheet

The first line decides whether he reads the second one. If it opens with what he's done — the money, the lies, the missed shifts — he stops reading and starts building his defense. So open with a moment that proves you see him, not just the problem: "I keep thinking about the summer you rebuilt that bike in the garage." An opening like that can't be argued with, and it lowers the guard the rest of the letter needs to get through.

The structure that carries every strong letter

After the memory, the letter moves in a simple order: what you've watched change, said as observations rather than conclusions ("you don't come to Sunday dinners anymore" — not "you've become an addict"). Then your fear, named specifically — not "I'm worried about you," but "I'm afraid of the call I might get some morning." Then one clear ask, small enough to be answerable. And finally the door, left visibly open: whatever he decides tonight, he hasn't lost you. Memory, change, fear, ask, door — in that order, on one page.

A real example

Alex,

I've started this letter four times. Every version sounded like a lecture, and you've had enough of those from me. So I'll just tell you the truth: last Tuesday, when you didn't answer until two in the afternoon, I sat in the driveway for ten minutes because I was afraid of what I'd find inside.

You're my son. I remember the summer you rebuilt that bike in the garage — you wouldn't come in for supper until the brakes worked. That's who I know you are. Someone who doesn't quit on hard things. I need you to know I can see how hard this one is.

I'm not writing this to list everything that's gone wrong this year. I'm writing because I'm scared, and because I love you more than I'm scared. What I'm asking is small: …

Notice what the letter has done before the ask even arrives: it admitted its own awkwardness, anchored to one specific morning, and reminded him who he is outside the addiction. What goes in the rest — the ask itself, and what happens if the answer is no — depends entirely on your son and your situation, which is exactly why copying someone else's ending doesn't work.

What to leave out

No inventory of every incident — he was there for all of them, and a list turns the letter into a trial. No labels or diagnoses; "you're an addict" gives him something to argue with, while "I've watched you disappear this year" doesn't. And no ultimatum you aren't fully prepared to keep — an empty one teaches him your words are negotiable, which is the opposite of what this letter is for. If he reads the letter and still insists there's no problem at all, that's its own conversation — here's what to say when they won't admit they have a problem.

Reading it versus sending it

If you can, read it to him out loud, in a calm and sober moment, with no audience — the letter keeps your voice steady when the moment gets heavy. If every face-to-face attempt so far has ended in a blow-up, sending it is not a cop-out; it's using the letter for what it does best. Either way, know your next move before he answers.
