How to Talk to Your Parent About Their Drinking (Without Crossing a Line)

TheFirstWord

Worrying about a friend or a sibling is one thing. This is your mother or your father — the person who taught you to drive, who signed your report cards, who still corrects you at the dinner table. You've watched the drinking get worse. And every time you think about saying something, the same thought stops you: who am I to tell my parent how to live?

The short version: saying something isn't disrespect. Watching it get worse and staying quiet isn't respect, either — it's fear.

You're not overstepping — the roles already shifted

The reason this conversation feels impossible is that it breaks a rule you've followed your whole life: parents worry about children, not the other way around. But if you're reading this, that rule is already broken — you've been worrying for months. Saying it out loud doesn't create the role reversal. The drinking did. You're just the first one honest enough to acknowledge it.

Expect the pushback to be about respect, not alcohol

When a spouse hears "I'm worried about your drinking," they argue about the drinking. When a parent hears it, they usually argue about the hierarchy: "I'm still your mother." "I was raising you before you could pour a glass." Don't take the bait. You're not claiming authority over them — you're claiming the right to be scared for someone you love. Try: "You're right, you're my dad. That's exactly why I can't watch this and say nothing." Go alone, or go coordinated — never as a surprise jury

If you have siblings, decide together who speaks — one voice, chosen deliberately, usually the one your parent gets least defensive with. Three adult kids showing up at once feels like an ambush, and a cornered parent defends their pride before they hear a word. Never do it at Christmas dinner or any family gathering: being confronted in front of the family they raised is humiliating, and humiliated people say no. And if your other parent tends to minimize it — or pours the wine themselves — don't count on them as an ally yet. Talk to your parent directly.

Talk about the last six months, not the last thirty years

Maybe the drinking isn't new. Maybe it's been at every family dinner since you were a kid. Doesn't matter — don't litigate the past, because they'll have a defense ready for every decade of it. Anchor to what changed recently: since retirement, since the funeral, since the diagnosis, since the falls started. "Dad, you've always liked your beer. But since you stopped working, it's different — and you know it's different." A recent change is much harder to deny than a lifetime everyone got used to.

Name the fears that come with their age

Vague worry slides right off a parent — they'll remind you they were managing fine before you were born. Specific fears don't slide: the wine mixed with blood-pressure medication. The stairs. Driving to the grocery store after the afternoon glasses. The long days alone in the house. Whether the grandkids will still be allowed to sleep over. Pick the two that are actually true in your family, and say them plainly.

Make the ask small — and let a doctor carry part of it

You can't send a parent to their room, and "you need treatment" from their child's mouth is easy for a parent to wave off. But "will you mention it to your doctor at your next appointment — or let me come with you?" is a different kind of ask. It's small, it's concrete, and it hands the next step to someone whose authority they already accept. Your job isn't to fix this in one conversation. It's to end the silence around it.
